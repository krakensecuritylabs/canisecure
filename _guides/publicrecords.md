Anyone who has been on the internet — or simply alive — for at least 20 years likely has publicly accessible records about themselves online. This is not something you opted into; it is the byproduct of having a digital life. Data brokers scrape public records, social media profiles, purchase histories, and other sources to build detailed profiles that anyone can search for a small fee or sometimes for free.

Completely erasing yourself from all public records is effectively impossible. The goal is to reduce your exposure enough that a casual search does not immediately surface your home address, phone number, family members, or other sensitive details.

## Understand What Is Out There

Before you start removing records, search for yourself to understand your current exposure.

**Search engines:** Google your full name (in quotes), your name plus your city, your phone number, and your email address. Check at least the first five pages of results.

**Google's "Results About You" tool:** Google now offers a built-in monitoring tool that scans search results for your personal contact information and alerts you when it appears. Access it at Settings → Results About You (or visit myactivity.google.com/results-about-you). You can request removal of search results that contain your home address, phone number, email address, or government ID numbers. Note that this removes results from Google Search but does not delete the underlying data from the source websites — you must opt out from those directly.

**People-search sites:** Visit a few of the major data broker sites listed below and search for yourself. You may be surprised at the detail — home addresses, estimated income, relatives, and even political affiliations.

## Social Media Audit

Public social media accounts are one of the richest sources of personal information. Review each account and remove anything that could be used against you, including photos of your home exterior, car, license plate, driver's license, boarding passes, and mail showing your address.

Go into each platform's Privacy Settings and set them to the least amount of public sharing you are comfortable with. If you are not actively using an account, consider deactivating or deleting it rather than leaving it dormant. Refer to the individual platform guides in this series (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, Twitter, Reddit) for detailed settings.

## Data Broker Opt-Out

Data brokers collect and sell your personal information. You can opt out from each one individually, though the process is time-consuming and must be repeated periodically since many brokers re-add your data over time.

### Manual Opt-Out List

Below are major data broker and people-search sites with opt-out options. Visit each site and follow their removal instructions. Most require you to find your listing first, then submit an opt-out request with identity verification.

- [National Do Not Call Registry](https://www.donotcall.gov/) — Reduces telemarketing calls. Register all your phone numbers.
- [Spokeo](https://www.spokeo.com/optout) — Search for your listing, copy the URL, and submit an opt-out request.
- [BeenVerified](https://www.beenverified.com/f/optout/search) — Search for your listing and follow the removal steps.
- [Intelius](https://www.intelius.com/optout) — Submit your listing URL for removal.
- [Instant Checkmate](https://www.instantcheckmate.com/opt-out/) — Search and request removal of your profile.
- [LexisNexis](https://optout.lexisnexis.com) — One of the largest commercial databases. Submit a consumer disclosure request.
- [Whitepages](https://www.whitepages.com/suppression-requests) — Search for your listing and request suppression.
- [Radaris](https://radaris.com/page/how-to-remove) — A major aggregator. Follow their removal instructions.
- [TruePeopleSearch](https://www.truepeoplesearch.com/removal) — Free site with easy removal form.
- [FastPeopleSearch](https://www.fastpeoplesearch.com/removal) — Search, find your listing, and submit removal.
- [PeekYou](https://www.peekyou.com/about/contact/optout/) — Submit your profile URL for removal.
- [FamilyTreeNow](https://www.familytreenow.com/optout) — Request removal of your listing.
- [MyLife](https://www.mylife.com/ccpa/index.pubview) — Submit a data deletion request.
- [Acxiom](https://isapps.acxiom.com/optout/optout.aspx) — Major marketing data broker. Opt out of their databases.
- [Oracle/Datalogix](https://www.oracle.com/legal/privacy/marketing-cloud-data-cloud-privacy-policy.html) — Opt out of Oracle's data marketplace.

This list is not exhaustive — there are hundreds of data brokers. Expect to spend several hours on your first pass, and plan to revisit every 90 days since many brokers re-list your information after removal.

### California DELETE Act and DROP Platform (2026)

If you are a California resident, you now have a powerful new tool. California's DELETE Act established the Delete Request and Opt-Out Platform (DROP), which launched in January 2026. Through DROP, you can submit a single authenticated deletion request to every registered data broker in California — eliminating the need to contact each one individually.

How it works: Visit privacy.ca.gov/drop, verify your identity, and submit one request. Starting August 1, 2026, data brokers must retrieve and process consumer deletion requests from DROP at least every 45 days. Non-compliant brokers face enforcement from the California Privacy Protection Agency, which launched a dedicated Data Broker Enforcement Strike Force in late 2025.

Even if you do not live in California, many data brokers are registered there and may honor DROP requests regardless of your state. It is worth submitting a request.

### State Privacy Laws

Beyond California, 18 or more US states now have consumer privacy laws that require businesses (including data brokers) to honor opt-out and deletion requests. If you live in states like Colorado, Connecticut, Virginia, Texas, Oregon, or Montana, check your state's privacy resources for additional rights.

## Automated Data Removal Services

If you prefer not to spend hours manually opting out every quarter, several paid services will handle the process for you. These services continuously scan data broker databases, submit removal requests on your behalf, and monitor for re-listing.

**Recommended services to evaluate:**

- **Incogni** — Covers 420+ data brokers. Audited by Deloitte. Approximately $96 per year. Sends recurring removals every 60–90 days.
- **DeleteMe** — One of the longest-running services. Covers approximately 750+ brokers (though many require custom requests). Approximately $104 per year for individuals.
- **Optery** — Tiered pricing starting at approximately $39 per year (365+ brokers) up to $249 per year (535+ brokers with priority handling). Offers a free scan to show your current exposure before you pay.
- **Kanary** — Checks against 1,000+ data broker and people-finder sites. Approximately $180 per year.

No single service covers every data broker, but any of these will dramatically reduce your exposure compared to doing nothing. Results typically take a few months to materialize as brokers process removal requests on their own timelines.

We are not endorsing any particular service. Evaluate them based on your budget, the number of brokers covered, and whether they offer a free initial scan so you can see your current exposure.

## Beyond Data Brokers

### Voter Registration Records

In many US states, voter registration information (name, address, party affiliation) is publicly accessible. Some states allow you to request confidentiality — check with your county registrar or secretary of state. If your state does not offer confidentiality, consider using a PO Box or UPS Store address for voter registration if permitted.

### Property Records

If you own property, your name and address are on public deed records. Some people use an LLC or trust to hold property, which adds a layer of separation between your name and your address. Consult a real estate attorney if this is a concern.

### Court Records

Court filings, including civil lawsuits, divorces, and criminal records, are often publicly accessible through state court websites (like PACER for federal courts). These are generally not removable, but being aware of what is out there helps you prepare.

### Data Breach Exposure

Check whether your email addresses and passwords have appeared in data breaches by visiting [Have I Been Pwned](https://haveibeenpwned.com). If your information appears, change the affected passwords immediately and ensure you are not reusing them elsewhere. See the Passwords guide in this series for passphrase best practices.

## Ongoing Maintenance

Removing your public records is not a one-time task. Data brokers continuously scrape and re-aggregate information from public sources.

- **Quarterly:** If you are managing removals manually, revisit the major data broker sites every 90 days and re-submit opt-out requests as needed.
- **Annually:** Re-search your name, phone number, and email in Google and major people-search engines to check for new listings.
- **After life events:** Moving, getting married or divorced, buying property, or changing your phone number all generate new public records. Re-run your removal process after major life changes.
- **Google monitoring:** Keep the "Results About You" tool active so Google alerts you when new results containing your personal information appear.

## Quick-Start Checklist

1. Google yourself and review the first five pages of results
2. Set up Google's "Results About You" monitoring
3. If you are a California resident, submit a request through the DROP platform at privacy.ca.gov/drop
4. Either manually opt out of the major data brokers listed above, or sign up for an automated removal service
5. Register all your phone numbers on the National Do Not Call Registry
6. Audit your social media accounts for publicly visible personal information
7. Check Have I Been Pwned for breach exposure
8. Set a quarterly calendar reminder to review and repeat
